The act

The sun sets. The garden begins to glow.

On a hillside in northern India, a meditation retreat has eight small black boxes screwed into eight stone walls. Nobody walks the property at sundown to switch anything on.

One by one, at the right minute for the right latitude, the paths warm. The pond lights breathe up. The shrine takes its cue from the shrine next door, not from a server in Frankfurt.

When a guest walks the corridor at three in the morning, the lights notice, brighten the way, and then, with the politeness of a host who would rather not be seen, dim back down.

A campus is a small kingdom. It should not need a foreign embassy to turn on its own lights.

— the premise
How it works

Four moves, in order.

  1. 01

    Sense

    Time of day, sunrise and sunset for the actual latitude, motion in the corridor, the ambient light outside.

  2. 02

    Decide

    Manual override beats motion beats schedule beats the default state. The arbiter doesn’t consult anyone else.

  3. 03

    Drive

    Eight constant-current LED channels, dimmed smoothly from zero to one hundred. Two relays for the loads that don’t dim.

  4. 04

    Weave

    The unit murmurs its state to its neighbours over LoRa, picks up theirs, and they stay in step without a single packet leaving the property.

The unit

One PCB. One firmware. A role chosen at boot.

Every box on the wall is the same. Up to eight per campus, each wired to its own set of fixtures. One of them is told, at the time of configuration, that it is the coordinator; the rest are leaves.

The coordinator hosts a small web dashboard for the venue manager to tune schedules and push updated configs out to the leaves. If the coordinator’s WiFi falls over, the leaves keep their schedules and keep going. Nobody’s evening is ruined by an ISP outage.

A small honesty

Lokki is not a smart-home platform. It will not greet you by name, play music, or learn your habits. It will not, ever, ask you to download an app.

It runs the lights, listens for motion, watches the sky, and stays out of the way. That is the entire promise.

The name is aloka — Pali for radiance — folded through a Norse vowel and a Finnish accent.

Limits

What lokki can’t do.

  • Replace an electrician. It speaks at low voltage. The mains side is still someone’s licensed problem.
  • Cover a city. One coordinator, up to seven leaves. Beyond a campus, you want a different kind of system.
  • Survive without a roof. The enclosure is indoor-rated. Outdoor fixtures yes, outdoor electronics no.
